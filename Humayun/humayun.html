Humayun Ahmed

A Literary Luminary of Bangladesh

Humayun Ahmed (1948-2012) is an iconic figure in Bangladeshi literature, whose contributions span across novels, short stories, dramas, and films. His unique narrative style and deep understanding of human emotions have made him a beloved author among readers of all ages.


Early Life and Education

Born on November 13, 1948, in Mohanganj, Netrakona, Humayun Ahmed grew up in a family that valued education and culture. His father, Foyzur Rahman Ahmed, was a police officer, and his mother, Ayesha Foyez, was a homemaker. Humayun showed an early interest in literature and science, a dual passion that would later influence his diverse body of work.

Humayun completed his secondary education at Bogra Zilla School and higher secondary education at Dhaka College. He went on to earn his bachelor's and master's degrees in Chemistry from the University of Dhaka. Later, he obtained his PhD in Polymer Chemistry from North Dakota State University in the United States.

Literary Career

His debut novel, "Nondito Noroke" (In Blissful Hell), published in 1972, marked the beginning of a prolific career with over 200 published works. Ahmed's writing, characterized by its simplicity, humor, and emotional depth, includes beloved characters like Himu and Misir Ali, who have become cultural icons in Bangladesh.

Humayun also made significant contributions to television and cinema. His popular TV dramas like "Ei Shob Din Ratri" and "Bohubrihi," as well as acclaimed films like "Aguner Parashmoni" and "Srabon Megher Din," showcase his storytelling prowess.

Despite personal challenges, including a battle with cancer, Humayun continued to write and inspire until his death on July 19, 2012. His legacy endures through his books, films, and the lasting impact he had on Bangladeshi literature and culture.
